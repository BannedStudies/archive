**Course Description**  
What goes into a creative project? This seminar and workshop will guide the student through the process of theorizing, researching, and proposing a creative work that is influenced by the field of cultural studies. By combining the workshop format with the seminar, Cultural Studies for Creative Professionals will teach students how to make artworks that are in dialogue with certain positions and arguments in the humanities. As a class, we will position ourselves between two ways of knowing, and learning to work as  “theory/practitioners,” to borrow Holly Willis’ term.[^1] Standing at the inbetween affords us, as Julain Bleeker, a professional designer who works at this same bridge, argues both affords the “vernacular and the pragmatic. . . because it can operate with wit and paradox and a critical stance.”[^2]  
But how do we create a work that is both aesthetic and critical, that draws inspiration from the world and from cultural theory? This class presents a broad overview of the cultural studies field, with an emphasis on more contemporaneous issues. Rather than emphasizing the spectres that haunt the history of the field, we concern ourselves with concerns of identity, death, memory, and agency. Focusing on a crisis in the humanities that comes as a result of late capitalism, the increasing terror of global climate change, and a rejection of the humanist position in the enlightenment, this course engages with, and reveals the theoretical problems that intersect in contemporary cultural studies.  
By engaging with these concerns, this class will ask the student to both engage critically, but also creatively and aesthetically to attempt to find practical ways of knowing and doing through subjects that interest the students. By the end of the semester, the student will have a beginning knowledge of a discourse surrounding one of their interests, tools to be able to continue research in that field, and a working proposal to apply for grants for as well as create a medium sized artistic project.

**Course Objectives**

1) Students will get a brief primer on interests in cultural studies related to identity, agency, and subject formation. This will focus on more contemporaneous sources with the interest in equipping students with a vocabulary to address issues from a variety of viewpoints.  
2) Students will gain research skills, to better read and analyze academic texts, to better find sources for their own work, and to better critique those sources.  
3) Students will learn to draft and plan creative projects iteratively, and with the support of their peers. They will learn how to respond to critique and feedback, and incorporate it into their future drafts.  
4) Students will learn the process of previsualizing a new media, mixed media, installation, or film work. The final proposal will be designed to facilitate applications for grants, and should serve as the template for a future work.

**Class Format**  
We will meet twice a week, with each of our meetings functioning as either a seminar, workshop, or object lesson. While our main interests are in incorporating cultural studies into our creative work, it is necessary to work with the material in different ways.   
Every week will have one **seminar**, a discussion led by the professor to cover key points in every article, to help distill the information for the students. These will be conversations, where students should bring up their concerns, their criticisms, their confusion with the texts in question. Given the difficulty of these texts, and the breadth of material we are covering this semester, these texts will be applied to the next session’s workshop or object lesson class.  
**Workshops** are, as in the tradition of creative teaching, designed for students to get a hands on approach to the ideas in class. Some of these workshops will involve addressing key material in the previous week, and others are larger stepping stones toward the final project. Each workshop will require some form of work to be completed in advance—a writing exercise, a draft of part of the proposal, a pitch. For each of these sessions, we will be learning how to talk about these topics in the context of our own work. While the credit for each of these small assignments stands for the majority of the final grade, this will be determined more so by the completeness of the assignment, and not necessarily the quality. *These projects are drafts and will be graded accordingly*.  
In addition to these first two class types, we will occasionally have **object lessons**. These classes are meant to learn how to apply difficult material to creative works, to either reverse engineer how a work came together, to better explain a creative object, or to explain the theoretical material more fully. Some of these lessons will involve reading ahead of time, but will usually only mean one or two chapters of additional work for the week. In the few times we have screenings, we will meet on Monday evenings to screen the films.

**Readings**  
As part of a seminar, students are expected to read the material in full, and be ready to talk about it class. We will be spending a good deal of time during the semester working on academic reading skills, and it is paramount to the kind of practice this class intends to teach. A large portion of the participation credit will depend on being able to participate during the seminar discussions, as a lively discussion is very important to facilitating the learning of this complicated material.

*A Side Note:* It is not expected that you will have full comprehension of these materials. We read for seminar as part of a beginning process of learning a text. This means that being confused, or having questions about a certain text is as necessary as being able to talk about it. Please bring these concerns to the class, and we can work through them as best we can over the day’s session.

**Workload**  
This class is designed to require about 5 hours of work outside of class each week (roughly 2 hours of reading and 3 hours of writing on average). It has been designed to work iteratively towards a proposal, and each of the assignments will continue to build towards that final project. As such, some weeks might be too much to handle. 

While I expect on time completion of the material, I understand that this is a learning environment. If you are having trouble please talk to me. I want to help in any way I can.

**Course Assessment**  
In Class Participation — 20% of Final Grade  
Students are expected to come to class prepared to discuss the assigned texts, and be ready to respond to their peers’ creative work. It is vital for the class for our discussions to be active, as we will use the seminar time to work through difficult theoretical texts. Students will be asked to participate at least once during each class period (I will be taking notes), as it is important to learn to speak in an academic seminar space.  
I know it is difficult at times to be able to participate in these environments, and that some students have trouble voicing their opinions, but for the sake of professional development I believe it is important to get out of one’s comfort zone and engage with the discussion.  
If you are having trouble with this, I am always happy to speak in private during my office hours, or at a time that best suits you. 

Weekly Assignments — 50% of Final Grade  
The implicit goal of these weekly assignments are to get your final proposal in order. Since the final proposal is creative work, and it is very difficult to grade creative work without personal bias being involved, a majority of the grade has been placed on the iterative elements leading to the final project. The majority of each assignment’s grades will be placed on whether the assignment is done on time, and if it fulfills the minimum requirements expected.  
I am not expecting perfect work for any of these step by step assignments; instead, the design takes into consideration small, iterative steps with an emphasis on meeting deadlines, and working through problems as they arise.  
Again, if you are having trouble with this aspect of the class, please feel free to message me, and we can talk during my office hours.

Final Proposal — 20% of Final Grade  
Students will turn in a proposal for a medium sized artistic project (a 10-20 minute film, a series of artworks, a small installation). For this proposal, the following materials will be required:  
Artist Statement: 1-2 Pages  
This essay will explain who you are as an artist. Where do you come from? What drives your creative work? Who inspires you? Why are you doing this project? Tell us a story about your life, how you got here, and where you plan to go with this project.  
		Introduction: 2-3 Pages  
At this point you have introduced yourself. Now you will introduce the project. Why are you making it? With whom are you working? What are the specific things the reader needs to know before they get to the synopsis?

Synopsis: At least 5 pages (Floorplans, sketches, previsualizations also work.)  
Describe the project in detail. This means that if you are making a documentary, you should be able to tell the story you are thinking of making, and describe how it would cut together. If you are making a narrative film, you should have a script. If you are doing an installation, you should describe what is there, along with detailed floorplans and sketches.  
		Intellectual Rationale: At least 10 Pages  
Take the material we have been working on, and the material you have been reading in addition to this course and synthesize it. Who do you agree with? What ideas make you think about your art? What do you disagree with? In what way does the scholarly work inform or contradict the work you make?  
		Budget  
How much will the project cost. I am not looking for a detailed, exhaustive budget, but I would like you to think through the materials necessary, the personnel needed, and the kind of expenses that would be necessary to finish the work.
